More Incidents & Techniques

Key Lessons from Major Attacks

WannaCry – Unpatched services → global incidents. Disable legacy protocols. Backups essential.
NotPetya – Supply-chain software is a powerful compromise point. Ransomware-looking attacks can be wipers.
SolarWinds – Build pipelines and signing keys are high-value targets. Monitor trusted updates.
Colonial Pipeline – Single-factor remote access is a major risk for critical infrastructure.

Generic Attack Techniques

SQL Injection – Unsafe queries; data disclosure, privilege escalation.
XSS (Cross-Site Scripting) – Injected scripts; session hijacking, defacement.
Phishing & Spear-Phishing – Deceptive messages; account takeover, malware entry.
Credential Stuffing – Reused passwords from breaches; automated account takeover.
Insider Threats – Misuse of legitimate access; data theft, sabotage.

Who Attacks and Why

Common Threat Actors

  • Nation-state groups – Strategic, political, military goals
  • Cybercriminal gangs – Organised crime, money at scale
  • Ransomware affiliates – Ransomware-as-a-service operators
  • Hacktivists – Political/social motivation, visibility
  • Insiders – Employees, contractors misusing access
  • Script kiddies – Ready-made tools, leaked exploits

Who Attacks and Why

Typical Goals

  • Financial gain – Extortion, fraud, theft of funds or data
  • Espionage – IP, trade secrets, sensitive communications
  • Disruption – Services offline, chaos, pressure
  • Reputation damage – Leaks, defacement
  • Physical impact – Industrial systems, energy, transport, healthcare
  • Strategic influence – Public opinion, trust in institutions

How Organisations Defend

Before an Attack (Prevention)

  • Patch systems and applications
  • Enforce MFA and strong authentication
  • Least privilege for accounts and services
  • Segment networks
  • Email filtering and web protection
  • EDR/anti-malware, secure baselines
  • Staff training on phishing and social engineering

How Organisations Defend

During an Attack (Detection & Response)

  • Monitor logs (endpoints, servers, identity, cloud)
  • SOC / central monitoring
  • Incident response runbooks
  • Isolate suspicious systems
  • Preserve evidence for forensics
  • Communicate with leadership, legal, stakeholders

After an Attack (Recovery & Learning)

  • Restore from clean backups
  • Rotate credentials, keys, tokens
  • Root-cause analysis, post-incident review
  • Improve controls, monitoring, training

Life of a Cyber Attack

  1. Reconnaissance – Research targets, exposed systems, credentials
  2. Initial Access – Phishing, exploits, stolen passwords
  3. Persistence – Backdoors, scheduled tasks
  4. Privilege Escalation & Lateral Movement – Credentials, internal exploits
  5. Actions on Objectives – Data theft, encryption, disruption
  6. Detection, Containment & Eradication – Defenders respond
  7. Recovery & Improvement – Restore, strengthen, learn

Frequently Asked Questions

Vulnerability vs exploit vs attack?

Vulnerability = weakness. Exploit = how it’s abused. Attack = campaign using exploits.

Are all attacks highly technical?

No. Many rely on people: phishing, social engineering. Culture and training matter.

How can small orgs protect themselves?

Patch, MFA, backups, awareness. Small improvements dramatically reduce risk.

How often are attacks attributed?

Often uncertain. Investigators use infrastructure, tooling, behaviour; attackers can fake these.

Glossary

Malware
Malicious software to damage, disrupt, or gain access.
Ransomware
Encrypts data, demands payment to restore.
Phishing
Tricking people via deceptive messages.
DDoS
Overwhelming a service with traffic.
Zero-day
Unknown vulnerability, no patch yet.
Supply-chain attack
Compromising a supplier to reach many targets.
MITM
Intercepting/altering communication between two parties.
Incident response
Organised approach to detecting and handling incidents.
CIA triad
Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability.
