    Who Attacks and Why

    Understanding the people behind attacks and their motivations is key to building realistic defences.

    Common threat actors

    • 🏛️Nation‑state groups – well-funded teams pursuing strategic, political or military goals.
    • 💰Cybercriminal gangs – organised crime focused on making money at scale.
    • 🔐Ransomware affiliates – semi‑independent operators using "ransomware as a service".
    • ✊Hacktivists – politically or socially motivated groups seeking visibility and impact.
    • 👤Insiders – employees, contractors, or partners misusing legitimate access.
    • 🧒Script kiddies – individuals using ready‑made tools and leaked exploits.

    Typical goals

    • 💵Financial gain – extortion, fraud, theft of funds or monetisable data.
    • 🔍Espionage – stealing intellectual property, trade secrets, or sensitive communications.
    • ⚡Disruption – taking services offline to cause chaos or pressure decision‑makers.
    • 📰Reputation damage – leaking embarrassing data or defacing websites.
    • 🏭Physical impact – targeting industrial systems, energy, transport, or healthcare.
    • 🗳️Strategic influence – shaping public opinion or undermining trust in institutions.

    How Organisations Defend

    Defence is a continuous cycle of prevention, detection, response, and learning.

    Before

    Prevention

    • Keep systems and applications patched and up to date.
    • Enforce strong authentication, ideally multi‑factor authentication (MFA).
    • Use the principle of least privilege for accounts and services.
    • Segment networks so one breach does not expose everything.
    • Deploy email filtering and web protection against phishing.
    • Harden endpoints with EDR/anti‑malware and secure configuration baselines.
    • Train staff on phishing, social engineering, and safe data handling.
    During

    Detection & Response

    • Monitor logs across endpoints, servers, identity, and cloud platforms.
    • Use a SOC or central monitoring to correlate alerts.
    • Have clear incident response runbooks for different attack types.
    • Isolate suspicious systems quickly to limit spread.
    • Preserve evidence for forensics while containing the threat.
    • Communicate early with leadership, legal, and affected stakeholders.
    After

    Recovery & Learning

    • Restore from clean, tested backups and verify integrity.
    • Rotate credentials, keys, and tokens that may be exposed.
    • Conduct root‑cause analysis and a formal post‑incident review.
    • Improve controls, monitoring, and training based on lessons learned.
    • Update playbooks, asset inventories, and recovery procedures.
    • Share anonymised insights with trusted communities where possible.

    Life of a Cyber Attack

    Most attacks follow a recognisable lifecycle. Mapping activity to these stages helps defenders detect and respond earlier.

    1. 01

      Reconnaissance

      Attackers research targets, identify exposed systems, leaked credentials, and potential weak points.

    2. 02

      Initial Access

      First foothold is gained, for example through phishing, exploiting a vulnerability, or abusing stolen passwords.

    3. 03

      Persistence

      Attackers install backdoors, scheduled tasks, or modify configurations to survive reboots and basic cleanup.

    4. 04

      Privilege Escalation & Lateral Movement

      They steal credentials, exploit internal flaws, and move between systems to reach high‑value assets.

    5. 05

      Actions on Objectives

      Actual goals are executed: data theft, encryption for ransom, service disruption, or manipulation of systems.

    6. 06

      Detection, Containment & Eradication

      Defenders identify suspicious activity, isolate affected systems, remove malware, and close exploited paths.

    7. 07

      Recovery & Improvement

      Systems are restored, controls are strengthened, and the organisation improves its resilience for the next attempt.

    Rankings & Future Outlook

    These rankings reflect common industry understanding, not precise global statistics. They help prioritise where to focus limited time and budget.

    Overall risk ranking today

    Rank Attack type Frequency Typical business impact Attacker success vs defence Financial loss Why it ranks here
    1 Phishing & credential theft Very high Account takeover, data exposure, ransomware entry High – humans remain easy to trick High but variable Cheap to run, scales globally, bypasses many technical controls.
    2 Ransomware & extortion High Full service outage, data loss, reputational and legal impact Medium–high Very high per incident Industrialised ecosystem, targets backups and restores, hits every sector.
    3 Business email compromise (BEC) High Fraudulent payments, supplier fraud, long investigations Medium–high High Combines social engineering with weak processes around payments and approvals.
    4 Web app & API exploitation Medium–high Data breaches, service outages, regulatory exposure Medium High for data‑heavy organisations Many organisations still lack secure SDLC and basic testing.
    5 Supply‑chain compromises Lower volume Wide blast radius across many organisations at once Medium Extremely high in worst cases Compromise one supplier to reach hundreds of downstream targets.

    Loss per successful incident

    1. Supply‑chain compromise of core software – can impact thousands of customers and critical infrastructure.
    2. Ransomware on critical operations – halts production, healthcare, or logistics for days or weeks.
    3. Large‑scale data breach – regulatory fines, notification, identity protection, and brand damage.
    4. Business email compromise of finance – direct theft through fraudulent transfers.
    5. Targeted industrial control (ICS/OT) attack – physical damage, safety impacts, long recovery cycles.

    Success vs loss "return" for attackers

    • Phishing + credential stuffing – low cost and highly automated; even a small success rate is profitable.
    • Ransomware with data theft – combines encryption with extortion, increasing pressure to pay.
    • Fraud and BEC – minimal infrastructure required; relies on social engineering and poor process controls.
    • Cloud misconfiguration abuse – easy wins where security settings are left open by mistake.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    Clarifying common concepts and concerns when first learning about cyber attacks.

    What is the difference between a vulnerability, an exploit, and an attack?

    A vulnerability is a weakness in a system or process. An exploit is the specific way that weakness is abused. An attack is the overall campaign or action that uses one or more exploits to achieve a goal, such as stealing data or encrypting systems.

    Are all cyber attacks highly technical?

    No. Many successful attacks rely more on people than technology: phishing emails, fake login pages, social engineering phone calls, or tricking someone into changing payment details. Technical defences matter, but culture, training, and process controls are just as important.

    How can small organisations with low budget protect themselves?

    Focus on a few high‑value basics: keep devices patched, use reputable cloud services, turn on MFA wherever possible, back up critical data, and practise what to do if something goes wrong.

    How often are attacks properly attributed to specific groups or countries?

    Attribution is difficult and often uncertain. Some campaigns are publicly linked to particular groups or states, but many incidents are never formally attributed. Investigators look at infrastructure, tooling, language, targets, and behaviour, but attackers can reuse and fake these signals.

    Where can I learn more after this hub?

    Good next steps include vendor neutral resources, national cyber security centres, and community projects summarising real incidents. Look for material that explains ideas in plain language and uses real‑world examples.

    Glossary

    Malware
    Malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorised access to systems.
    Ransomware
    Malware that encrypts data or locks systems, demanding payment to restore access.
    Phishing
    Tricking people into revealing information or performing actions via deceptive messages.
    DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service)
    Overwhelming a service with traffic from many sources so legitimate users cannot access it.
    Zero‑day
    A vulnerability that is unknown to the vendor and has no official patch at the time of exploitation.
    Supply‑chain attack
    Compromising a trusted supplier or component to reach many downstream organisations.
    MITM (Man‑in‑the‑Middle)
    Intercepting and potentially altering communication between two parties without their knowledge.
    Incident response
    The organised approach to detecting, handling, and learning from security incidents.
    CIA triad
    Core security objectives: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability of information and systems.
